Advanced operations demand the **mechanisms** behind the weather — so you can anticipate
hazards rather than just react to them.

## Moisture & stability

- Warm air holds more moisture. As air is lifted and cooled to its **dew point**, cloud
  forms; the **lapse rate** describes how fast temperature falls with height.
- **Unstable air** keeps rising once lifted → **cumulus build-ups, turbulence, showers,
  and thunderstorms**.
- **Stable air** resists rising → **stratus cloud, haze, and calm** but possibly poor
  visibility.

## Fronts

A **front** is where two air masses meet. As a low approaches, a **warm front** usually
precedes a **cold front**. **Cold fronts** push under warm air and shove it up quickly —
making the air **unstable** and triggering the most vigorous weather.

## Thunderstorms

The most dangerous weather you can meet:

- Powerful **updrafts and downdrafts**, **microbursts** and **gust fronts** create sudden
  severe wind shear — often **well outside** the visible storm.
- Add hail, lightning and rapid darkening. **Avoid flying within ~15 NM.**

<Caution>
A storm's **gust front** can hit with damaging wind long before the cloud overhead — and
its outflow can roll over your site from a storm that still looks distant.
</Caution>

## Reading the forecast

- **METAR** — an actual surface weather **report**.
- **TAF** — an aerodrome **forecast**. Use both (plus AWOS/LWIS) to build the picture.

<Note>
Memory aid: **METAR = what it's doing now; TAF = what it's expected to do.**
</Note>

## Key takeaways

- **Unstable air → cumulus, turbulence, storms; stable air → stratus, haze.**
- **Cold fronts** lift warm air fast and bring the worst weather.
- Thunderstorms = updrafts/downbursts/**microbursts**; **stay ~15 NM clear**.
- Combine **METAR (report)** and **TAF (forecast)** before you decide.
